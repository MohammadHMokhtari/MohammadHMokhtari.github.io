Research in Cancer Immunology

Understanding the intricate dialogue between the immune system and cancer cells

The Foundation of My Work

My research focuses on tumor immunology — specifically the complex interplay between immune-checkpoint pathways and cancer cell survival. The immune system possesses remarkable capabilities to recognize and eliminate malignant cells, yet cancer has evolved sophisticated mechanisms to evade this surveillance.

During my training, I explored how Rogaratinib affects PD-1/PD-L1 expression in PBMCs co-cultured with MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells. These studies deepened my interest in immune-regulatory mechanisms and the potential of immunotherapy to reshape cancer treatment. The PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint pathway represents one of cancer's most elegant escape mechanisms, and understanding how to modulate it therapeutically opens new avenues for patient care.

💡

Key Research Question

How can we harness the body's own immune system to recognize and destroy cancer cells more effectively, while minimizing harm to healthy tissue?

Immune Cell Dynamics

I am particularly fascinated by the roles of Natural Killer (NK) cells and macrophages in modulating anti-tumor responses. NK cells serve as the immune system's rapid response force, capable of eliminating malignant cells without prior sensitization. Macrophages, meanwhile, exhibit remarkable plasticity — they can either promote or inhibit tumor growth depending on their polarization state and the tumor microenvironment.

Understanding how these immune cells interact with cancer cells, and how the tumor microenvironment influences their behavior, is crucial for developing more effective immunotherapeutic strategies. The tumor microenvironment is not merely a passive scaffold but an active participant in cancer progression and immune evasion.
